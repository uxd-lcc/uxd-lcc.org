Aims & Context

This unit introduces you to the foundational practice of graphic design and specifically the practice of information visualisation and typography. You will be introduced to how formal graphic elements such as geometric forms and shapes are combined to produce meaningful visual messages in the broader context of visual communication.

This context requires that you engage with visual research and iterative creative practice and that you take these lessons and processes forward to all subsequent units on the course. Central to working in a design-led context is an understanding of the relationship of form to content.

Brief: Timeframes

Design and produce two static visualisations from within the same timeframe.

These two visualisations should work together to allow your audience to understand your chosen timeframe. This may be by showing two complementary views, or it may be by contrasting or even contradicting each other.

When choosing your timeframe, you should consider different scales or dimensions of time - planetary epochs, empires, eras of civilisation, lifetimes, governments, years, holidays, working weeks, performances, feature films, phone calls, conversations, heartbeats, blinks, frames, refresh rates, beats per minute, sample rates, atomic vibrations.

Specifications

The visualisations can be of any dimensions, but consider how their size, shape and fidelity suits your audience and topic. They may fit comfortably on screen, or they may require scrolling, panning, and zooming in order to fully experience and explore.

You should consider who your visualisations are for. You will be expected to demonstrate an understanding of your intended audience, and the rationale for how you have implemented your idea.

In designing your visualisations, consider the following pairs of questions:

Are they readable as a whole? Or Does they require detailed inspection?

Do they need a key, instructions? Or are they immediately obvious?

If your subject or information is complex, are you trying to show its complexity? Or are you simplifying it?

Neither approach is necessarily better than the other, but you should consider and be able to explain why you would choose one over the other.

Learning Outcomes

The unit learning outcomes and how they can be evidenced
On completion of this unit you will be able to: You will show evidence of this by:
Demonstrate an awareness of working in a design-led context toward a specified outcome using appropriate processes and methods to record research and the development of ideas and concepts. (Enquiry) The research you include in your portfolio of work will show how you have approached the topic, what sources you have drawn on and how the research journey has informed your design.
Demonstrate the ability to use visual research to inform project outcomes. (Enquiry) Your submitted portfolio of work and research blog will show how you have used visual research methods such as sketching, and diagramming. This will demonstrate your understanding of the relationship between visual methods and design output.
Demonstrate an awareness of fundamental principles of graphic design, information visualisation and typography. (Knowledge) Your submitted portfolio of work will show how you have implemented an understanding of colour relationships, spatial relationships and perceptual principles in design
Experiment with a variety of formal graphic structures, materials and media to enhance meaning and communication whilst considering the social effects and environmental impact of these decisions. (Process) You will show how you have taken risks and worked in new forms to expand your awareness of the techniques of typography, data visualisation and graphic design. You will document and analyse your experiments in your submitted portfolio of work.
Present visual solutions to peers and articulate your visual thinking. (Communication) You will use your design skills to present your work. Your visual thinking will be evident in how you articulate the aims of your work using visual means. Your visual and conceptual development of these presentations will be shown in your portfolio of work.
Demonstrate an ability to engage with directed visual production and manage your time. (Process, Realisation) Your submission and blog posts will demonstrate how you have developed your ideas throughout the unit and the process you have used to produce your work. You will reflect on your own progress and personal time management in this unit.

Assessment Information

Assessment requirements

This unit is assessed on a pass/fail basis. You will receive written feedback but no grade will be assigned to your work.

Portfolio of work: documenting the outcomes for the set project work. This will also include a process blog and/or sketchbook documenting your iterative design and development process specifically in the areas of graphic design and information visualisation.

This unit is assessed holistically (100% of the unit). Assessment will be against the specified marking criteria, the UAL site has assessment guides, policy and regulations
